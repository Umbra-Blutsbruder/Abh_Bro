The Test Of Life

In this age of our world, we have learned that, to gain success, you must pursue only material gains and focus on materialistic goals in general. I am not talking about buying toys or eating luxurious meals that you can survive without. I am talking about the secular men who cry and blame God for the death of their beloved, or the men who blame God because they have lost their job, marriage, house, or whatever God had given them before they lost it. As God says in Surah "Hūd," verses 9 to 11:

"If We give people a taste of Our mercy then take it away from them, they become utterly desperate, ungrateful.

But if We give them a taste of prosperity after being touched with adversity, they say, “My ills are gone,” and become totally prideful and boastful,

except those who patiently endure and do good. It is they who will have forgiveness and a mighty reward."

Men understand the world in a way that makes them believe that something they possess in their hands truly belongs to them. You control nothing but your intentions and the actions you can commit. Everything is a test from God; otherwise, it would not have existed. When somebody exclaims, "God is not merciful, how could they take away my beloved!?" remember: "Have you considered what you sow? Have you seen that [seed] which you sow? Is it you who makes it grow, or are We the grower?" From Surah Vakia, verses 63–64.

You did not create your beloved, nor did you allow your beloved to exist. What God does for your beloved is entirely in the hands of God. If God wills it, we could all have lived without death, but what test is that? If you crave heaven on earth, you are following the guidebook of Satan, for you cannot have heaven on earth, which Satan promises.

The emotions you feel after somebody dies, do you not think that God made those emotions for a reason? Do you not think that feeling those emotions is a test in and of itself? We must carefully maneuver through this life and follow the guidebook that God, the All-Sustainer, gave us, which is to endure hardship, be patient, and do good. Those are the successful.

Death is natural, and death caused by other humans is the test of all of us: the one experiencing the death, the one committing the evil act, and the observers. All of them will feel emotions, and God will judge them based on what they do with those emotions. When patience is mentioned, it does not mean to stand idly, for you would have supported such an evil act if you stand and watch. Instead, as Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said in his last Friday sermon: "Do not oppress and do not be oppressed." One of his famous Hadith sheds light on what to do if you see evil: "I heard the Messenger of Allah (ﷺ) say, 'Whosoever of you sees an evil, let him change it with his hand; and if he is not able to do so, then [let him change it] with his tongue; and if he is not able to do so, then with his heart, and that is the weakest of faith.'"

In case of hardship, do not be purely stoic, for Stoicism teaches you that your life has no inherent meaning and nothing has inherent meaning, so why should you feel angry when you are not in control anyway? In practice, Islam and Stoicism seem similar, but the philosophy behind the same actions has vastly different foundations. In Stoicism, if you experience a great disaster, you are not in control of it anymore, so why should you make it worse? They view virtue as the only true good in life, but it has no ultimate meaning beyond itself. If you do good, you do not expect anything in return, so while it might motivate some people to do good, it is not as convincing to many people as it is in Islam. Some Stoics might say, "Our meaning is virtue. Virtue itself is the meaning. Good is the only meaningful thing." But both good and evil, as actions, are to an indifferent universe the same: one takes and the other gives. It is like an ocean taking a stone away and giving a stone back; the beach does not care. In that indifferent universe, both deeds are the same. Thus, even secular virtue is ultimately meaningless, since you will die, everybody dies, and that is the end of that, not much meaning, if any.

Islam is different in that, after you have done an action and no longer have control over it, it is in the hands of God. It is not random chance. A great difference between the two is that, in Islam, all types of suffering bring you good deeds. Even if you are sick, your sins get expiated because you suffer at the hands of nature, remain patient, and glorify God. Yet that is also a test. If you become a transgressor in the eyes of God because of the burden of your illness, you are failing the test. The only excused awake adults are the ones who have an illness of the mind and thus are not rational.

There are many who do have this mindset, but there are plenty of secular people who think life is meaningless, and thus whatever they do means nothing. Therefore, they transgress while they hide and do good while they are watched. We must increase our remembrance of God, as God says, "And when My servants ask you [O Prophet] about Me, indeed I am near. I respond to the invocation of the supplicant when he calls upon Me. So let them respond [with obedience] to Me and believe in Me, so they will be guided."

Establish prayer often and feed the poor, as referenced in these ayats:

Surah Al-Baqarah (2:43): "And establish prayer and give zakah and bow with those who bow [in worship and obedience]".

Surah Taha (20:14): Allah's direct command to Prophet Moses (and believers): "...establish prayer for My remembrance".

Surah Al-Ankabut (29:45): Highlights the spiritual benefit: "...establish prayer. Indeed, prayer prohibits immorality and wrongdoing".

Surah al-Ankabut (The Spider) explains it best. When you lose motivation after a night's sleep, pray, and you will remember the sweetness of success, and you will find yourself striving to do good.

Enjoin good and forbid evil.

Peace be with you.
